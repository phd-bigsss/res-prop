Social Comparison, subjetive social status and preferences for redistribution

Chapter 2 Redistributive preferences

During the past decade, the research field of individual preferences toward economic redistribution constitute an interdisciplinary field in the social sciences (A. Alesina & Giuliano, 2010; Steele & Breznau, 2019).

In this regard, as a result of disciplinary boundaries, the phenomena has been addressed through a range of concepts, such as attitudes toward redistribution [García-Sánchez et al. (2019);@], social or welfare policy preferences (N. Breznau, 2010; Rehm, 2016) and preferences, support or demand for redistibution (M. Evans & Kelley, 2018; Jaime-Castillo & Marqués-Perales, 2019; Steele et al., 2022).

Preferences are defined as how individuals think and tend to choose regarding to an object evaluated (Janmaat, 2013)

There are slight distinctions between the approaches mentioned above. On aspect is the entity that is in charge about how resources, in this case, are redistributed. On the one hand, it is possible to evaluate and prefer a global reduction of inequality (preferences toward global redistribution). On the other hand, it is possible to distinguish global inequality reduction from welfare policies that are generally focused in specific dimensions of social inequality, such as education, health or unemployment (preferences toward redistributive policies). These can be focalized or universal social policies.

Other aspect is the specific object, in this case, policies that are being evaluated by the observator.

A theoretical relevant aspect of how to understand this phenomenon is to disentangle the underlying dimensions that comprise it.

  • Global reduction of inequality [ISSP]
  • Government social policies []
  • Tax regime/policy [tax]
  • Allocation or recipient / focalization [welfare]

The literature on redistributive preferences has proposed different mechanisms for explaining the demand for redistribution.
